üö® Decompression Sickness (DCS) - The Bends

Decompression sickness occurs when nitrogen dissolved in body tissues during diving comes out of solution too quickly during ascent, forming dangerous bubbles.

TIME IS CRITICAL: Early treatment dramatically improves outcomes. Any suspected DCS requires immediate recompression therapy.

Type I DCS (Mild) - Joint Pain/The Bends

Joint & Muscle Pain

  • Pain in major joints (shoulders, elbows, knees, ankles)
  • Muscle aches and soreness
  • Usually localized pain
  • Pain gradually increasing
  • Onset: 15 min to 12+ hours post-dive

Skin & Lymph Issues

  • Rash or mottled skin appearance
  • Swollen lymph nodes
  • Itching or burning sensations
  • Skin marbling (severe)
  • Lymph node enlargement

Type II DCS (Serious) - Neurological/Pulmonary

Neurological (Spinal Cord)

  • Paralysis or weakness in legs
  • Tingling or numbness
  • Loss of bowel/bladder control
  • Severe back pain
  • Onset: Minutes to hours

Respiratory (Pulmonary/Chokes)

  • Chest pain or tightness
  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
  • Cough or wheezing
  • Requires IMMEDIATE emergency response
  • LIFE-THREATENING condition

Central Nervous System

  • Loss of consciousness
  • Disorientation or confusion
  • Dizziness or vertigo
  • Difficulty speaking
  • Vision problems

DCS Response Protocol - Step by Step

1
RECOGNIZE SYMPTOMS - Post-Dive
Any pain, numbness, weakness, paralysis, or breathing difficulty after diving = SUSPECT DCS
2
ALERT EMERGENCY SERVICES - IMMEDIATELY
Call 911 or emergency number. State: "Diving emergency - suspected decompression sickness" and location.
3
GIVE 100% OXYGEN (if available)
Place oxygen mask on patient immediately while awaiting ambulance. Oxygen speeds nitrogen elimination from body.
4
POSITION PATIENT - Keep Horizontal
Lay patient flat (supine position). Prevent movement. Monitor breathing and consciousness. Record time of symptom onset.
5
DO NOT RE-PRESSURIZE (without medical direction)
Do not attempt in-water treatment. Patient must be transported to hyperbaric chamber for proper treatment.
6
CONTACT DAN (Divers Alert Network)
Call DAN emergency hotline: 919-684-9111 (24/7). They locate nearest chamber and provide medical guidance.
7
TRANSPORT TO HYPERBARIC CHAMBER
Arrange immediate transport. Time from symptom onset to recompression is critical for recovery.
8
RECOMPRESSION TREATMENT - USN-5 or USN-6
Trained hyperbaric technician and physician conduct recompression protocol. Patient stays in chamber under medical supervision.
9
POST-TREATMENT MONITORING
Extended oxygen therapy, hydration, medical evaluation. NO DIVING for extended period (weeks to months minimum).

‚ö° Oxygen Toxicity (Ventilation/VENTOX-C)

Oxygen toxicity occurs when breathing oxygen at high partial pressures (PO‚ÇÇ > 1.6 bar). Causes central nervous system (CNS) toxicity and manifests as sudden convulsions or disorientation.

CRITICAL: CNS oxygen toxicity can strike without warning. Symptoms may progress rapidly from subtle to life-threatening.

Early Warning Signs (VENTOX-C Mnemonic)

V Vision: Tunnel vision, blurred vision, difficulty focusing
E Ear: Tinnitus (ringing in ears), hearing difficulties
N Nausea: Upset stomach, queasiness, dizziness
T Twitching: Muscle twitches, lip twitching, facial spasms
O Oxygen: Chest pain, difficulty breathing, coughing
X-C Convulsions/Consciousness: Seizures, loss of consciousness, disorientation

Oxygen Toxicity Response Protocol

1 IMMEDIATE ACTION: STOP OXYGEN
Switch to air breathing gas immediately. Do not continue oxygen breathing.
2 REDUCE DEPTH
Ascend to shallower depth to reduce partial pressure of oxygen.
3 NOTIFY DIVE SUPERVISOR
Report symptoms immediately. Supervisor initiates emergency protocol.
4 ABORT DIVE
Begin controlled ascent following decompression obligations for actual bottom time on air.
5 MEDICAL EVALUATION
Once at surface, diver must receive medical evaluation. Monitor for delayed symptoms.
6 DIVING RESTRICTION
No diving until medically cleared. CNS toxicity incidents require investigation and modifications to future protocols.

Rapid Ascent / Uncontrolled Ascent

1
Inflate BC / Establish buoyancy control
If using equipment, dump weight and inflate buoyancy control device.
2
Ascend to surface at controlled rate (< 10 m/min)
Do not drop weights unless absolutely necessary. Control breathing.
3
Surface - Alert support team
Signal distress, request assistance, get out of water when possible.
4
Medical evaluation and observation
Monitor for DCS symptoms for 24+ hours post-incident. Be prepared for recompression if symptoms develop.

Out of Air / Low Pressure

1
Signal team immediately
Use hand signal: hold hand to throat and slash across neck.
2
Buddy breathing or emergency ascent
If buddy available: share air source. If solo: emergency ascent at controlled rate.
3
Control ascent rate
Ascend slowly to surface. Continue breathing if possible. Do not hold breath.
4
Surface and immediate medical evaluation
Report incident, get medical check-up, monitor for complications.

Loss of Consciousness / Disorientation

1
Support diver - prevent water inhalation
Secure diver, protect airway, maintain horizontal position in water.
2
Controlled ascent to surface
Inflate support equipment, manage buoyancy, maintain slow ascent rate.
3
Surface - Immediate CPR if needed
Call emergency services, begin CPR if no pulse/breathing, prepare for evacuation.
4
Hospital transport - Treat as DCS until proven otherwise
Give 100% oxygen. Monitor vital signs. Assume diving emergency (DCS or AGE).

‚úì Recompression Treatment Overview

Recompression is the definitive treatment for decompression sickness. It works by:

  • ‚Ä¢ Reducing bubble size through increased pressure (Boyle's Law)
  • ‚Ä¢ Restoring blood flow to affected tissues
  • ‚Ä¢ Accelerating nitrogen elimination from body through breathing oxygen
  • ‚Ä¢ Reducing inflammation and immune response

  • ‚Ä¢ Oxygen toxicity monitoring: Technician watches for CNS symptoms during treatment
  • ‚Ä¢ Hydration critical: IV fluids given to promote nitrogen elimination

Why Early Treatment is Critical

< 1 Hour
90%+ recovery rate for full resolution of symptoms
1-6 Hours
70-80% recovery with possible residual effects
6-24 Hours
50% or less recovery - significant disability risk
> 24 Hours
Permanent neurological damage likely - emergency still warranted
