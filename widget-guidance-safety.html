Understanding OTU (Oxygen Toxicity Units)

OTU measures cumulative oxygen exposure over time. Each dive profile contributes OTU based on depth, duration, and oxygen percentage.

Nitrogen Narcosis Management

Nitrogen Narcosis Effects by Depth

  • 0-30m: No significant effects; slight euphoria possible
  • 30-40m: Mild narcosis; slight coordination loss, delayed reaction times
  • 40-60m: Moderate narcosis; impaired judgment, poor fine motor control
  • 60-80m: Severe narcosis; disorientation, inability to perform complex tasks
  • >80m: Critical narcosis; extreme disorientation, unconsciousness risk

Management: Commercial diving typically limited to 60m air maximum. Nitrox reduces narcosis. Proper training and experience essential.

Proper table selection is critical for diver safety. The following factors determine which decompression table to use.

Dive Technique Decision Tree

1. Can diver ascend directly to surface without stops?

‚úì Yes - Use ND15 Table

No-stop air dives where bottom time stays within table limits. Direct ascent permitted.

‚úó No - Continue below

Decompression stops required - choose between in-water, surface, or bell decompression.

2. Will decompression occur in-water only?

‚úì Yes - Use SIL15 Table

Standard air decompression with all stops in-water. No bell or chamber required. Most economical.

‚úó No - Surface/Bell decompression

Diver will surface or use bell for decompression. Requires oxygen capability and surface support.

3. Will oxygen be used during decompression?

‚úì Yes - Use SOX15 or BOX15

SOX15: Surface decompression with oxygen (shallow stops on air, surface stops on oxygen)
BOX15: Bell-based oxygen decompression with extended capability.

‚úó No - Use SAB15 (backup)

Air-only surface decompression table. Use only if oxygen system fails. Not for primary planning.

4. Is nitrox gas being used?

‚úì Yes - Use NIA15 or NIB15

NIA: 40% nitrox (18-27m max depth, reduced nitrogen narcosis, faster decompression)
NIB: 35% nitrox (18-33m, deeper capability)

‚úó No - Use standard air table

Continue with SIL15, SOX15, or other air-based tables based on dive profile.

Pre-Dive Briefing Topics

  • Dive objectives and time limits: Why, where, when, how long
  • Depth and decompression: Exact depths, bottom time, stop requirements
  • Gas usage and contingencies: Primary and backup breathing gases, switch criteria
  • Navigation and signals: Entry/exit location, navigation plan, communication signals
  • Buddy procedures: Stick together, check on each other, assist procedures
  • Emergency procedures: Abort criteria, ascent procedure, emergency signal, rescue
During-Dive Safety

Key Points to Remember

  • Monitor time and depth continuously - use computer or watch + depth gauge
  • Check buddy regularly - at intervals determined by plan
  • Ascend slowly - maintain 5-10 m/min ascent rate throughout dive
  • Follow decompression stops exactly - do not skip or shorten stops
  • Watch for signs of problems - pain, disorientation, shortness of breath, unusual sensations
  • Stop dive and ascend if objectives met or safety concerns arise
  • Breathe normally - do not hold breath during ascent
  • Conserve air - monitor pressure gauge, manage consumption rate

üè• Medical Fitness for Diving

Certain medical conditions make diving unsafe. Divers must be in good health to safely handle the physical and physiological demands of commercial diving.

Medical Screening - Conditions That Prevent Diving

Absolute Contraindications

The following conditions make diving unsafe:

  • Heart conditions: History of heart attack, angina, cardiac surgery, arrhythmias
  • Lung disease: Asthma, emphysema, history of collapsed lung, cystic fibrosis
  • Neurological: Seizure disorders, cerebral palsy, multiple sclerosis
  • Ear/Sinus: Perforated eardrum, chronic otitis, severe sinus disease
  • Diabetes: Insulin-dependent diabetes (some programs allow non-insulin)
  • Pregnancy: Diving prohibited during pregnancy
  • Alcohol/Drug dependency: Active substance abuse disqualifies from diving
  • Recent surgery/injury: Generally must wait 6+ weeks before resuming diving

Recovery After Diving

Post-Dive Care Recommendations

  • Rest: Allow 24 hours minimum between last dive and flying/high altitude travel
  • Hydration: Drink plenty of water to help nitrogen elimination
  • Avoid alcohol: Do not consume alcohol for 24 hours after diving
  • Monitor symptoms: Watch for any DCS signs in first 24 hours post-dive
  • Light activity: Gentle activity OK, but avoid strenuous exercise
  • Ear care: Keep ears dry, avoid water until dry
  • Log dive details: Record depth, time, decompression, any incidents
  • Rest before next dive: Allow minimum surface interval per plan

Dive Limits & Safety Margins

Conservative Planning Practice

Always build in safety margins and plan conservatively:

  • Bottom time: Plan 5-10 minutes less than table allowance
  • Depth: Plan for slightly shallower than maximum intended depth
  • Ascent rate: Plan for slowest acceptable rate (5-7 m/min ideal)
  • Oxygen exposure: Stay 10% below OTU daily limit
  • Contingency gas: Always carry reserve air - never dive on exact calculations
  • Repetitive dives: Use maximum interval between dives when possible

‚≠ê Excellence in Commercial Diving

These practices represent the highest standards of professional diving safety and competence.

Culture of Safety

Principles for Safe Operations

  • Speak up: Report concerns, near-misses, and unsafe conditions immediately
  • Stop unsafe dives: Never proceed if conditions aren't right - safety over schedule
  • Ask questions: If you don't understand something, ask before diving
  • Share knowledge: Help others learn from your experience
  • Continuous improvement: Always look for ways to make operations safer
  • Respect experience: Learn from veteran divers and supervisors
  • Take responsibility: Own your decisions and their consequences
Physical Fitness & Health

Maintain Diving Fitness

  • Cardiovascular fitness: Regular aerobic exercise improves oxygen efficiency
  • Strength training: Core and leg strength helps with equipment management
  • Flexibility: Stretching reduces injury risk and improves comfort
  • Mental health: Address stress, anxiety, depression - talk to professionals
  • Sleep quality: 7-9 hours nightly is crucial for decision-making and safety
  • Nutrition: Balanced diet supports body's decompression processes
  • Avoid smoking: Reduces lung capacity and increases DCS risk
  • Annual checkups: Regular medical evaluations confirm continued fitness
Continuous Learning

Professional Development

  • Advanced certifications: Pursue technical diving credentials and specializations
  • Table mastery: Become expert in decompression and dive planning
  • Emergency medicine: Take first aid and CPR courses regularly
  • Equipment knowledge: Thoroughly understand all gear used in your operations
  • Incident analysis: Study near-misses and accidents to learn and improve
  • Stay current: Keep up with latest safety research and industry standards
  • Mentoring: Teach newer divers - reinforces your own knowledge
Decision-Making Framework

Before Every Dive - STOP & THINK

Use this framework to make safe decisions:

  • S - Stop: Pause before proceeding. Take time to evaluate.
  • T - Think: Consider all aspects - conditions, plan, team, contingencies.
  • O - Optimize: Can we make this safer? Better conditions? Different approach?
  • P - Proceed or Postpone: Either proceed with confidence or postpone safely.
